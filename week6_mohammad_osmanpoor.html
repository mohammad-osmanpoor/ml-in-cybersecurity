✍ Answer Of Question¶

Based on the extended epsilon range up to $10^2$, the plot shows several key observations about the clustering performance metrics. The Davies-Bouldin score, which measures cluster separation quality (lower is better), continues to decrease as epsilon increases, stabilizing at its lowest values around $10^2$. This indicates that clusters become increasingly well-defined as privacy constraints are relaxed. The Adjusted Rand Score, which measures alignment with ground truth labels (higher is better), shows a steady improvement with increasing epsilon, peaking near 0.65 at $10^2$. This suggests that the clustering results align more closely with the actual labels as noise diminishes. The Average Inertia, which measures cluster compactness (lower is better), exhibits a dramatic drop as epsilon increases, stabilizing at its lowest value near $10^2$, indicating highly compact clusters with minimal privacy noise.

The trends can be explained by the mechanism of differential privacy. At very low epsilon values ($<1$), significant noise is added to ensure strong privacy guarantees, leading to unstable and degraded clustering performance. As epsilon increases beyond 1 and approaches $10^2$, the added noise becomes negligible, and the differentially private K-means algorithm behaves almost identically to the standard K-means algorithm. This results in stable and optimal clustering performance across all metrics. The stabilization of metrics at higher epsilon values highlights the diminishing returns of relaxing privacy constraints beyond a certain threshold; further increases in epsilon yield minimal improvements in utility.

In conclusion, while strong privacy guarantees ($\epsilon < 1$) significantly impact clustering performance due to added noise, moderate to high epsilon values ($1 \leq \epsilon \leq 100$) strike a balance between privacy and utility. For practical applications, choosing an epsilon value in this range depends on specific privacy requirements and acceptable utility levels.
