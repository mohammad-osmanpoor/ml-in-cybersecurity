Question 1: What is underfitting and overfitting?¶

Underfitting occurs when a machine learning model is too simplistic to capture the underlying patterns or relationships in the data. This typically happens when the model has insufficient complexity (for example a linear model applied to a highly non-linear problem) or when it is undertrained (not enough training iterations). As a result, the model performs poorly on both the training data and the test data, showing high error rates. For example, in the spam filtering task, an underfitted Naive Bayes model might fail to distinguish between spam and ham messages because it cannot learn the nuanced word patterns that differentiate the two classes.

Overfitting, on the other hand, occurs when a model is overly complex and fits the training data too closely, capturing not only the true patterns but also the noise and outliers specific to the training set. This leads to excellent performance on the training data but poor generalization to unseen data (test set), as the model fails to adapt to new variations. In the context of the spam filtering task, an overfitted Naive Bayes model might memorize specific words or phrases in the training messages (for example "win a prize" in spam) without learning general patterns, leading to incorrect classifications on new messages that dont contain those exact phrases.

Question 2: How underfitting and overfitting relate to high bias and high variance?¶

Underfitting is directly related to high bias. Bias refers to the error introduced by overly simplistic assumptions in the model. A high-bias model, such as a linear classifier applied to a complex dataset, assumes a structure that doesnt match the true data distribution, leading to systematic errors. In the spam filtering task, a high-bias Naive Bayes model might assume that all words are equally indicative of spam or ham, ignoring important word probabilities, resulting in underfitting and poor accuracy on both training and test sets.

Overfitting is associated with high variance. Variance refers to the models sensitivity to small changes in the training data. A high-variance model, such as one with too many features or overly tuned parameters, will change its predictions significantly when trained on different subsets of the data. In the spam filtering task, if the Naive Bayes model overfits by focusing too heavily on rare words in the training set (for example a specific spam message with unusual phrasing), it will have high variance, performing well on the training data but poorly on the test set because those rare words may not appear in new messages. The goal is to balance bias and variance to achieve good generalization, as seen in the task where the optimal alpha for Laplace smoothing is determined to maximize the area under the ROC curve.

#removing stopwords and Data Cleaning
import re

def decontracted(phrase):
    # specific
    phrase = re.sub(r"won't", "will not", phrase)
    phrase = re.sub(r"can\'t", "can not", phrase)

    # general
    phrase = re.sub(r"n\'t", " not", phrase)
    phrase = re.sub(r"\'re", " are", phrase)
    phrase = re.sub(r"\'s", " is", phrase)
    phrase = re.sub(r"\'d", " would", phrase)
    phrase = re.sub(r"\'ll", " will", phrase)
    phrase = re.sub(r"\'t", " not", phrase)
    phrase = re.sub(r"\'ve", " have", phrase)
    phrase = re.sub(r"\'m", " am", phrase)
    return phrase

from bs4 import BeautifulSoup
preprocessed_message = []

for sentance in data['message'].values:
    sentance = re.sub(r"http\S+", "", sentance)
    sentance = BeautifulSoup(sentance, 'lxml').get_text()
    sentance = decontracted(sentance)
    sentance = re.sub("\S*\d\S*", "", sentance).strip()
    sentance = re.sub('[^A-Za-z]+', ' ', sentance)
    sentance = ' '.join(e.lower() for e in sentance.split() if e.lower() not in stopwords)
    preprocessed_message.append(sentance.strip())
